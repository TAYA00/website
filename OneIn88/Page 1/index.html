Better health for everyone, everywhere

We are building a better, healthier future for people all over the world.Working with 194 Member States, across six regions, and from more than 150 offices, our staff are united in a shared commitment to achieve better health for everyone, everywhere.Together we strive to combat diseases — communicable diseases like influenza and HIV, and noncommunicable diseases like cancer and heart disease.

Our Mission and Value

Our mission is to assist and support those who are affected by autism, to reach their full potential, and improve their quality of life, increase awareness of the problem

  • promoting the most effective and advanced therapy through providing training for the parents/caregivers, therapists;
  • creating and introduce new technology to make life easier for those who affected by autism and those who effected by autism;
  • increase awareness of autism and related challenges
  • autism does not discriminate against nationality, or social status; therefor, we are focusing on those who need the most.

Help us a society that works for autistic people

One in 54 US children have autism, one in 140 cannot speak. iPads have become a revolutionary tool in teaching children with autism. This technology helps students communicate, finally giving them a «voice.»

Stories of real people

I am a single mother of a 5 year old boy who was diagnosed with autism. After my child was diagnosed, I was depressed and having daily panic attack for about 2 years. That’s when Breathwork came into my life. My life was changed. I found peace and stopped having a panic attacks. My mission now is to help other parents using breathwork and meditation to find balance and happiness in their life again. A year ago, I started my education at the “Academy of Breath and Meditation AirA” and now I am a certified teacher.

—Anna Smichkova Ventures ATL Employee

Understanding Risk Factors and Causes of ASD

Understanding the risk factors that make a person more likely to develop autism spectrum disorder (ASD) will help us learn more about the causes. CDC is currently funding and working on one of the largest U.S. studies to date, called Study to Explore Early Development (SEED).

SEED will help identify factors that may put children at risk for ASD andother developmental disabilities. SEED is a multi-year study being conductedat six sites and a data coordinating center, called the Centers for Autismand Developmental Disabilities Research and Epidemiology (CADDRE) network.
